Free SEO Audit: How to Check Your Website's SEO Health

May 21, 2026 · Textools Blog

Search engine optimization is not a set-it-and-forget-it task. Search algorithms change, competitors improve their content, and your own site evolves over time. Running a regular SEO audit is the only way to understand how your website actually performs in the eyes of search engines. Whether you manage a personal blog, an e-commerce store, or a corporate site, a thorough audit reveals exactly what is working and what needs attention.

The good news is that you do not need expensive enterprise software to run an effective audit. With the right approach and a few free tools, you can check every major SEO factor yourself. In this guide, we will walk through a complete SEO audit process that you can perform in under 30 minutes using Textools and other free resources.

What Is an SEO Audit and Why Do You Need One?

An SEO audit is a systematic evaluation of your website against search engine best practices. It covers multiple dimensions including on-page elements like titles and headings, technical factors like page speed and mobile responsiveness, content quality metrics, and off-page signals like backlinks. The goal is to identify gaps and opportunities that, when addressed, can improve your search rankings and organic traffic.

Without regular audits, small issues can compound into significant problems. A missing meta description on your most popular page, a broken internal link, or overly long title tags might seem minor on their own. But when a search engine crawler encounters several such issues, it may lower your site's perceived quality and push your rankings down. Running a monthly or quarterly audit keeps these problems in check and helps you maintain a healthy, competitive website.

Another reason to audit regularly is that search engines themselves change frequently. Google alone makes thousands of algorithm updates every year. A practice that boosted rankings last year may no longer be effective or could even harm your performance now. By auditing consistently, you stay aligned with current best practices and avoid penalties from outdated tactics.

Step 1: Check Your Meta Tags and Headings

Meta tags are the first thing search engines and users see about your pages. The title tag appears as the clickable headline in search results, while the meta description provides a brief summary beneath it. Getting these elements right is fundamental to good SEO because they directly influence click-through rates.

To audit your meta tags effectively, start by making a list of your most important pages — your homepage, top content pieces, product pages, and landing pages. For each one, check the following:

  • Title tag length: Keep titles between 50 and 60 characters. Longer titles get truncated in search results, which hurts readability and click-through rates.
  • Meta description length: Descriptions should be between 150 and 155 characters. Use a character counter to verify each description falls within this range. Descriptions that are too short miss an opportunity to convey value, while ones that are too long get cut off.
  • Keyword placement: Your primary keyword should appear near the beginning of both the title tag and meta description naturally. Avoid keyword stuffing — write for humans first.
  • Uniqueness: Every page should have a unique title and description. Duplicate meta tags confuse search engines and dilute your relevance.
  • Heading structure: Each page should have one H1 tag that matches or closely relates to the title. Subheadings (H2, H3) should follow a logical hierarchy and break up the content into scannable sections.

Step 2: Evaluate Your URL Structure

URL structure is a frequently overlooked but critically important SEO factor. A well-structured URL tells both users and search engines what a page is about before they even click. Clean, descriptive URLs also improve the user experience and make your site more shareable.

When auditing your URLs, look for these common issues:

  • Unnecessarily long URLs: URLs that contain multiple parameters, dates, or overly detailed paths are harder to read and remember. Aim for concise, meaningful slugs.
  • Stop words and filler: Words like "and," "the," "of," and "for" can often be removed from URL slugs without losing meaning. For example, /blog/how-to-use-tools is cleaner than /blog/how-to-use-the-tools-for-beginners.
  • Inconsistent naming conventions: Choose a format (lowercase with hyphens) and stick with it across your entire site. Mixed conventions confuse crawlers and look unprofessional.
  • Missing keywords: Your URL slug is a ranking signal. Include your primary keyword in the slug whenever possible without making it look spammy.

This is especially useful when you are publishing new content or restructuring existing pages. Good slugs also make it easier for other sites to link to yours, which builds valuable backlinks over time.

Step 3: Analyze Your Content Quality and Length

Content remains the most important ranking factor in SEO. Search engines prioritize pages that provide comprehensive, well-structured, and valuable information to users. During an audit, you need to assess whether your content meets current standards for depth, relevance, and readability.

Start by checking the word count of each important page. While there is no single perfect word count for SEO, studies consistently show that longer content tends to rank higher for competitive keywords. Blog posts between 1,500 and 2,500 words generally perform better than shorter pieces, provided the content is substantive and not padded with fluff. A word counter tool gives you an instant read on page length so you can quickly identify thin content that needs expansion.

Beyond length, evaluate content quality with these checks:

  • Topic relevance: Does the content fully address the search intent behind its target keywords? A page optimized for a question should answer that question directly and thoroughly.
  • Freshness: When was the content last updated? Search engines favor recently updated content for time-sensitive topics. Add a last-updated date to your articles and refresh them periodically.
  • Readability: Break up long paragraphs, use bullet points and numbered lists, and include subheadings that make your content easy to scan. Most readers skim before they commit to reading fully.
  • Internal linking: Link to other relevant pages on your site. Internal links distribute authority across your pages, help users navigate, and give search engines more paths to discover your content.

Thin, outdated, or poorly structured content directly harms your SEO performance. Each page on your site should have a clear purpose and deliver on it comprehensively. If a page consistently ranks poorly despite decent backlinks, the content itself is likely the weak link.

Step 4: Review Technical SEO Factors

Technical SEO covers the behind-the-scenes elements that affect how search engines crawl, index, and render your pages. Even the best content will struggle to rank if search engines cannot access it properly or if users have a poor experience loading it.

Here are the key technical factors to audit:

  • Page speed: Slow-loading pages frustrate users and trigger ranking penalties. Use Google PageSpeed Insights or similar tools to check load times. Aim for under three seconds on both desktop and mobile.
  • Mobile friendliness: With the majority of web traffic now coming from mobile devices, your site must work flawlessly on phones and tablets. Check that buttons are tappable, text is readable without zooming, and layouts adapt correctly to small screens.
  • Indexability: Check your robots.txt file and meta robots tags to ensure important pages are not accidentally blocked from search engines. Use the URL Inspection tool in Google Search Console to verify that your key pages are indexed.
  • Canonical URLs: If you have duplicate content (such as paginated category pages or HTTP and HTTPS versions of the same page), make sure canonical tags point to the preferred version to avoid dilution.
  • Image optimization: All images should have descriptive alt text that includes relevant keywords where natural. Compress images to reduce file size without sacrificing quality.

Technical SEO issues can be harder to spot without dedicated tools, but they often have the biggest impact on your overall rankings. Even fixing one or two technical problems can result in noticeable traffic improvements within weeks.

Step 5: Create an Action Plan from Your Findings

Running an audit is only valuable if you act on the results. After completing your checks, categorize each issue by severity and estimated effort. Some fixes, like updating a meta description or adding alt text to an image, take less than a minute. Others, like rewriting thin content or restructuring your site navigation, require more planning.

Prioritize your SEO action plan like this:

  1. Critical fixes (do immediately): Broken links, missing title tags, pages not indexed, duplicate meta tags, and server errors. These directly impact your ability to rank and be found.
  2. Important improvements (do this week): Short meta descriptions, overly long titles, thin content, missing alt text, and slow-loading pages. These are straightforward to fix and will improve both user experience and rankings.
  3. Ongoing optimization (schedule monthly): Content refreshes, new internal links, URL restructuring, and building new backlinks. These tasks maintain and grow your SEO momentum over time.

Keep a simple spreadsheet or document that tracks each issue, its status, and when it was resolved. Over time, this log becomes a valuable record of your SEO progress. You will be able to see which fixes moved the needle and which areas continue to need attention during future audits.

How Often Should You Audit Your Website?

The frequency of your SEO audits depends on the size and activity level of your site. For small blogs and personal websites, a quarterly audit is usually sufficient. For e-commerce sites, news publications, and any site that publishes content daily, monthly audits are recommended.

You should also run an ad hoc audit whenever you make significant changes to your site, such as redesigning pages, migrating to a new domain, restructuring your URL scheme, or launching a new section. Major updates can introduce new SEO issues that you want to catch early before they affect your rankings.

It is also worth running a quick audit after any significant algorithm update announcement from Google. Major core updates can shift ranking criteria substantially, and reviewing your site against the new landscape helps you adjust proactively rather than reactively.

Common SEO Audit Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced site owners make mistakes when auditing their own websites. Here are the most common ones and how to steer clear of them:

  • Auditing only the homepage: Your homepage is important, but it is only one page. Crawl your entire site to catch issues on deep pages that might otherwise go unnoticed.
  • Ignoring mobile performance: Desktop audits are incomplete. Always check how your pages perform on mobile devices separately, as the experience can differ significantly.
  • Focusing only on technical factors: Content quality and relevance ultimately drive rankings. A technically perfect site with mediocre content will not outperform a technically average site with excellent content.
  • Not documenting findings: Without a record of what you found and when, you cannot track improvement over time. Document each audit session and compare results from month to month.
  • Overreacting to minor fluctuations: Rankings naturally fluctuate. A small dip after an algorithm update does not necessarily mean your audit found the wrong things. Give changes time to stabilize before making drastic adjustments.

Avoiding these pitfalls ensures your audit time is well spent and that the insights you gather translate into real improvements in your search performance.
